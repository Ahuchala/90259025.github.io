Section 6: Inner Product Spaces

In linear algebra I, we first developed our theory of matrices and linear transformations for $\mathbb{R}^n$, and then brought them to more general vector spaces. Now that we’ve seen the dot product in $\mathbb{R}^n$, we’ll do the same to them to bring them to arbitrary vector spaces. The definitions of length, angle, and orthogonality could all be defined in terms of the dot product alone in $\mathbb{R}^n$, and so we’ll start there, trying to keep the definitions and their conditions to a minimum.

Definition: inner product

Let $V$ be a vector space. An inner product on $V$ is a function that takes two vectors $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{w}$ and outputs an element of $\mathbb{R}$, written $\left< \vec{v}, \vec{w} \right>$, such that:

1. It’s symmetric: $\left< \vec{v}, \vec{w} \right> = \left< \vec{w}, \vec{v} \right>$.

2. It’s bilinear: $\left< c\vec{u} + \vec{v}, \vec{w} \right> = c\left< \vec{u}, \vec{w} \right> + \left< \vec{v}, \vec{w} \right>$.

3. It’s positive definite: $\left< \vec{v}, \vec{v} \right> \geq 0$, and the only vector $\vec{v}$ for which $\left< \vec{v}, \vec{v} \right> = 0$ is $\vec{v} = \vec{0}$.

An inner product space is a vector space equipped with an inner product.

All of these properties were true of the dot product — in fact, they were the content of our first proposition about it — and so the dot product is the primordial example of an inner product. Let’s use general inner products to define the related concepts, and then we’ll take a look at some more exotic examples.

Definition: magnitude, angle, and orthogonality

Let $V$ be an inner product space. The magnitude (or length or norm) of $\vec{v} \in V$ is $\left| \left| \vec{v} \right| \right| = \sqrt{\left< \vec{v}, \vec{v} \right>}$. The angle between vectors $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{w}$ is

$$\begin{align*}\arccos\left( \frac{\left< \vec{v}, \vec{w} \right>}{\left| \left| \vec{v} \right| \right| \cdot \left| \left| \vec{w} \right| \right|} \right),\end{align*}$$

and we say that $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{w}$ are orthogonal if $\left< \vec{v}, \vec{w} \right> = 0$ (i.e. the angle between them is $90^\circ$).

Let’s look at the most common inner products on our usual vector spaces. On $\mathbb{R}^2$, for example, the typical choice is the standard dot product:

$$\begin{align*}\left< \left[\begin{array}{c} x_1 \\ y_1 \end{array}\right], \left[\begin{array}{c} x_2 \\ y_2 \end{array}\right] \right> = x_1x_2 + y_1y_2.\end{align*}$$

However, we could also replace the coefficients on $x_1x_2$ and $y_1y_2$ with any positive number, and the definition would still hold. If we tried to define

$$\begin{align*}\left< \left[\begin{array}{c} x_1 \\ y_1 \end{array}\right], \left[\begin{array}{c} x_2 \\ y_2 \end{array}\right] \right>_2 = -2x_1x_2,\end{align*}$$

making one coefficient negative and the other zero, then we fail positive definiteness:

$$\begin{align*}\left< \left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 0 \end{array}\right], \left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 0 \end{array}\right] \right>_2 = -2,\end{align*}$$

and

$$\begin{align*}\left< \left[\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 1 \end{array}\right], \left[\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 1 \end{array}\right] \right>_2 = 0,\end{align*}$$

even though that’s not the zero vector. On the homework, we’ll see that for any matrix $A$ with $A^T = A$ and all positive eigenvalues,

$$\begin{align*}\left< \vec{v}, \vec{w} \right> = \vec{v}^T A \vec{w}\end{align*}$$

defines an inner product on $\mathbb{R}^n$ (and in fact these are all the possible inner products on $\mathbb{R}^n$). Let’s look at some of the more exotic vector spaces, though.

Example: an inner product on polynomials

Let $V = \operatorname{span}\{1, x, x^2\}$ be a subspace of $\mathbb{R}[x]$. Show that

$$\begin{align*}\left< p, q \right> &= \sum_{n = 0}^2 p(n)q(n)\\[4px]&= p(0)q(0) + p(1)q(1) + p(2)q(2).\end{align*}$$

is an inner product, and find an orthonormal basis for $V$.

This definition is definitely symmetric. To show bilinearity, we have

$$\begin{align*}\left< cp(x) + q(x), r(x) \right> &= (cp(0) + q(0))r(0) + (cp(1) + q(1))r(1) + (cp(2) + q(2))r(2)\\[4px]&= cp(0)r(0) + q(0)r(0) + cp(1)r(1) + q(1)r(1) + cp(1)r(1) + q(1)r(1)\\[4px]&= c\left< p(x), r(x) \right> + \left< q(x), r(x) \right>,\end{align*}$$

and for positive definiteness,

$$\begin{align*}\left< p, p \right> = p(0)^2 + p(1)^2 + p(2)^2 \geq 0.\end{align*}$$

The last bit is somewhat more complicated — for $\left< p, p \right> = 0$, one possibility for $p$ is $p = 0$. But if we’re solving for $p$ given

$$\begin{align*}p(0)^2 + p(1)^2 + p(2)^2 = 0,\end{align*}$$

then we need

$$\begin{align*}p(0) &= 0\\[4px]p(1) &= 0\\[4px]p(2) &= 0,\end{align*}$$

which is a system of three equations and three unknowns (the coefficients of $p$). The resulting matrix is invertible, and so the only solution is $p = 0$.

To construct an orthonormal basis, we’ll follow the Gram-Schmidt process like we do in $\mathbb{R}^n$, but now the dot product is replaced with this strange new inner product. Let’s start with the basis $\left\{ 1, x, x^2 \right\}$ and work from there. We have

$$\begin{align*}\vec{v_1} &= 1\\[4px]\vec{v_2} &= x - \frac{\left< x, 1 \right>}{\left< 1, 1 \right>} 1\\[4px]&= x - \frac{0 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 1 + 2 \cdot 1}{1 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 1}1\\[4px]&= x - 1\\[4px]\vec{v_3} &= x^2 - \frac{\left< x^2, 1 \right>}{\left< 1, 1 \right>} 1 - \frac{\left< x^2, x - 1 \right>}{\left< x - 1, x - 1 \right>} (x - 1)\\[4px]&= x^2 - \frac{0 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 1 + 4 \cdot 1}{1 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 1}1 - \frac{0 \cdot (-1) + 1 \cdot 0 + 4 \cdot 1}{(-1) \cdot (-1) + 0 \cdot 0 + 1 \cdot 1}(x - 1)\\[4px]&= x^2 - \frac{5}{3} - 2x + 2\\[4px]&= x^2 - 2x + \frac{1}{3}.\end{align*}$$

Normalizing, we have an orthonormal basis of

$$\begin{align*}\left\{ \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(x - 1), \sqrt{\frac{3}{2}}\left( x^2 - 2x + \frac{1}{3} \right) \right\}.\end{align*}$$

Exercise: an inner product on continuous functions

Let $C[a, b]$ be the vector space of continuous functions $f : [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$. Show that

$$\begin{align*}\left< f, g \right> = \int_a^b f(x)g(x)\,dx\end{align*}$$

defines an inner product. With $X = \operatorname{span}\left\{ 1, x, x^2 \right\}$ as a subspace of $C[0, 1]$, find an orthonormal basis for $X$.

We’ll close with two fundamental results that relate the sizes of inner products to the magnitudes of their arguments.

Theorem: the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality

Let $V$ be an inner product space and $\vec{v}, \vec{w} \in V$. Then

$$\begin{align*}\left| \left< \vec{v}, \vec{w} \right> \right| \leq \left| \left| \vec{v} \right| \right| \cdot \left| \left| \vec{w} \right| \right|.\end{align*}$$

Theorem: the triangle inequality

Let $V$ be an inner product space and $\vec{v}, \vec{w} \in V$. Then

$$\begin{align*}\left| \left| \vec{v} + \vec{w} \right| \right| \leq \left| \left| \vec{v} \right| \right| + \left| \left| \vec{w} \right| \right|.\end{align*}$$
